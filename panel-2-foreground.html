Children in Maine’s Sardine industry

Young cutters. Photograph by Lewis Hine, Eastport, Maine, August 1911. Courtesy of the Library of Congress.

Beginning in 1875, entire families worked together in the canneries for “the season,” lasting from April to December. Most families came from local villages. Others gathered from a distance to live in company-owned camps. August launched the busy season. Boats loaded with herring arrived early each morning. Work in the busy season extended from dawn to well into the night. Maine’s 1907 Department of Labor reported the children could come and go from the work place as they wished, but many children worked long hours. While workers received decent wages, their income depended on “the catch.” The more fish, the more work. Companies paid workers by the piece, whether that meant filling a box of gutted fish or a can of processed sardines.
